CAP: Learning 3D Canonical Shape Representations Aligned with Human Preferences

PaperID:15333
Code Data Supplementary Material

TL;DR: A human-centric category-level point cloud canonicalization method that achieves SOTA consistency and aligns with human preferences. It exhibits improved physical significance, thereby driving numerous downstream tasks.

Abstract

Learning to predict the canonical shape representation of 3D objects is a crucial yet challenging task, as objects within the same category may exhibit significant variations. Meanwhile, previous canonical representations often significantly deviate from human preferences.

To achieve improved representation, we propose CAP, a human-centric, category-level point cloud canonicalization method that mitigates the impact of intra-category variations and aligns with human preferences. First, we design a Siamese Vector Neuron Module (SVNM), which enables SE(3) equivariant modeling and self-supervised disentanglement of shape and pose attributes. Next, we introduce an equivariant constraint to mitigate pose alignment biases caused by intra-category variations, thereby enhancing the consistency of the canonical representation. Unlike previous works, we introduce an upright dataset aligned with human preferences. By embedding real-world kinematic and geometric constraints, we learn residual rotations that align the neutral canonical representations with human preferences.

Experimental results demonstrate that CAP not only achieves SOTA consistency but also exhibits stronger physical interpretability, facilitating numerous downstream applications.

Overall architecture of CAP. \(X \rightarrow \widetilde{X}\): Learning neutral canonical shape representations of point clouds with arbitrary input poses. \(\widetilde{X} \rightarrow \widehat{X}\): Learning residual rotations to align the canonical shape representations with human preferences.

We offer a novel perspective： transforming in-the-wild, unaligned point cloud data into physically meaningful aligned datasets without relying on any pose annotations. In these datasets, the aligned axes are synchronized with the Euclidean coordinate system (e.g., airplane cockpits are always aligned with \( +\mathrm{Y} \), and car wheels always touch \( \mathrm{Z} = 0 \)).
